# References And Evidence

Apply this reference when deciding which inline links, citations, quotes, and numbers belong in a post — while drafting, and again as a dedicated trimming pass before publication.

btnopen posts are experience-led writing, not survey articles. A reference exists to support a claim the post depends on, not to demonstrate research effort. A draft that accumulates a citation for every paragraph reads as generated, and the links that matter drown among the ones that decorate.

## Reference Selection

Every inline link competes for the reader's attention. The test is whether removing the link would weaken a load-bearing claim — not whether the link is relevant to the topic.

**Examples:**

> Keep: the one study a paragraph's argument stands on; the workflow file the reader is told to copy; the original post being quoted.

> Cut: a second and third source that repeat the same point; a link on a passing mention; a "further reading" tangent; a citation for common knowledge.

**Guidelines:**

- MUST keep an inline reference only when it directly supports a load-bearing claim or is an artifact the reader is expected to open (a tool, a spec, a config the post discusses).
- MUST cut references that demonstrate research breadth, restate common knowledge, or decorate a passing mention.
- SHOULD keep one representative link when several sources make the same point, preferring the primary or most durable one.
- SHOULD prefer primary sources — the original post, paper, or official documentation — over secondary coverage.
- SHOULD treat a growing reference count as a cue to trim: the author's long posts settle around a dozen inline links, not dozens.
- MUST NOT add footnote-style asides — history or genealogy notes, trivia, adjacent research findings — unless removing them would cause a real reader misunderstanding.

## Strong Claims

Contested or surprising claims — employment effects, "X is no longer needed", productivity effects — earn trust through scoping, not through stacked citations. Scope the claim to where the evidence holds, then attach the single best source.

**Examples:**

> Better: 「書くための言語知識の価値は下がったが、レビューするための知識はむしろ重要になった」 with one study linked.

> Risky: an unscoped absolute claim followed by three studies and four statistics that all make the same point.

**Guidelines:**

- MUST scope a strong claim to where the evidence actually supports it before attaching any citation.
- MUST support each strong claim with the single best source, or at most two when they cover genuinely different aspects of the claim.
- MUST NOT stack multiple statistics that make the same point; pick the strongest one and cut the rest.
- SHOULD fold counter-evidence into the claim's qualifier instead of appending a both-sides survey paragraph.
- MUST keep the author's stated position intact; scoping exists to match the evidence, not to neutralize opinion.

## Quotes And Attribution

Quotation marks assert that wording is exact, and attribution asserts who said or made something first. Both are verifiable claims, and both are easy to get wrong when working from secondary coverage — a statement's viral moment and its origin are often different events.

**Guidelines:**

- MUST use verbatim quotation only when the exact wording has been verified against the original source.
- MUST attribute as paraphrase (「〜という趣旨」) with the speaker named when transcripts, translations, or secondary renderings vary.
- MUST verify origin claims — who coined, said, or published something first, and when — against primary sources before asserting them.
- SHOULD place the source link on the quotation itself rather than in surrounding prose.

## Numbers In Prose

A number in prose is a claim the reader may repeat elsewhere. Numbers that merely add texture dilute the ones that carry the argument.

**Guidelines:**

- MUST include a number only when it carries the argument, and link a verified source for it.
- MUST state direction instead of magnitude when an exact figure could not be verified against a primary source.
- SHOULD qualify numbers the way the source does — relative versus absolute, self-reported versus measured, snapshot date.
- SHOULD keep few enough numbers per post that each one stays memorable.

## Time-Sensitive Facts

Posts about fast-moving tools and ecosystems rot quickly. A fact verified at drafting time can be stale by publication, and an unanchored claim ages invisibly.

**Guidelines:**

- MUST re-verify time-sensitive claims — tool behavior, vendor support status, pricing, product names — at publication time, not only at drafting time.
- SHOULD anchor time-sensitive statements to a date or version (「2026年半ば時点では」, 「v1.56で」) so they age visibly.
- SHOULD confirm that linked in-repo artifacts, such as workflow files or configs, are publicly reachable before publishing.
